# Getting Started with Spelunk Vault: A Guide for Nonfiction Authors

Welcome to Spelunk Vault, a framework designed to help you manage the complex process of writing a nonfiction book — from early research through final manuscript. This guide will walk you through the structure of Spelunk Vault, explaining the purpose of each main directory and providing a roadmap for starting your next book project.

## The Spelunk Vault Philosophy

Spelunk Vault is built on the idea that nonfiction writing is fundamentally a research management problem. A well-organized system frees you to focus on the hard parts — finding the story in your material, constructing compelling arguments, and writing prose that keeps readers turning pages. It provides a home for every piece of your project: sources, interviews, outlines, drafts, correspondence, permissions, and the final manuscript.

The name "Spelunk" reflects the exploratory nature of nonfiction research — you're spelunking through archives, interviews, data, and ideas, bringing what you find back to the surface in organized form.

## Your First Steps

Getting started with Spelunk Vault is straightforward. Begin by exploring the structure. You will find that every directory has a purpose, and the included templates and examples will guide you in populating your vault. We recommend starting with the `10-Book-Bible/` to establish your thesis and scope, then moving to `20-Research/` to organize your source materials.

## The Directory Structure: Your Project at a Glance

Spelunk Vault uses a Johnny Decimal-inspired numbering system to organize your project into clear areas. The numeric prefixes ensure folders remain in a logical order, making navigation intuitive. For more on how Johnny Decimal works in this vault, see [[00-Introduction-to-Spelunk-Vault]].

| Directory | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- |
| **00-Project-Management** | Your mission control for scheduling, logging, and decision-tracking. |
| **10-Book-Bible** | The heart of your book: thesis, key arguments, audience, and scope. |
| **20-Research** | Your library: primary sources, secondary sources, interviews, data, bibliography. |
| **30-Development** | The workshop for proposals, outlines, and drafts in progress. |

## A Deeper Dive into Each Directory

### 00-Project-Management: Your Mission Control

This directory is the administrative heart of your project. It contains everything you need to manage your time, track progress, and maintain a consistent workflow.

- **System-Guide:** Core documentation for Spelunk Vault, including this guide.
- **Writing-Logs:** A place to record your daily or session-based writing progress.
- **Meetings:** Notes from editorial, research, and planning meetings.
- **Decisions:** A log of all major decisions about scope, structure, and content.
- **Schedules:** Writing calendars, deadlines, and project milestones.
- **Workflows:** Standard processes for research, writing, review, and fact-checking.

### 10-Book-Bible: The Heart of Your Book

The Book Bible is the single source of truth for what your book is about, who it's for, and what it argues. Before you dive into research and writing, this is the place to define your thesis, scope, and target reader.

- **Thesis:** Your central argument or premise, clearly stated.
- **Key-Arguments:** The major claims your book makes and the evidence supporting them.
- **Audience:** Who your reader is, what they already know, and comparable titles in the market.
- **Timeline:** A chronology of events relevant to your subject matter.
- **Glossary:** Definitions of key terms, jargon, and abbreviations.

### 20-Research: Your Library

This is the engine room of nonfiction writing. The Research directory houses everything you've gathered in service of your book — organized by type so you can always trace a claim back to its source.

- **Primary-Sources:** Original documents, firsthand accounts, raw data, government records, letters, diaries.
- **Secondary-Sources:** Published books, journal articles, news reports, analyses by other authors.
- **Interviews:** Transcripts, notes, and summaries from interviews you've conducted.
- **Field-Notes:** Observations from site visits, events, or immersive research.
- **Data:** Datasets, statistical analyses, charts, and quantitative materials.
- **Bibliography:** Your master bibliography, annotated bibliography, and citation style guide.

### 30-Development: Your Workshop

This is where the book takes shape. The Development directory is for the messy, iterative work of figuring out what your book will look like — from the initial proposal through multiple rounds of outlining and drafting.

- **Proposal:** The book proposal package: overview, chapter summaries, market analysis, author bio, sample chapters.
- **Outlines:** The evolving structure of your book, from high-level to chapter-by-chapter plans.
- **Drafts:** Working drafts and review copies, organized by stage.
- **Archive:** A home for old outlines, deprecated drafts, and earlier structural approaches.

### 40-Manuscript: The Final Product

Once a chapter is ready to leave the workshop, it moves to the Manuscript directory. This is where the book comes together as a unified, publication-ready document.

- **Front-Matter:** Title page, dedication, epigraph, table of contents, foreword, preface, acknowledgments, introduction.
- **Chapters:** The finalized chapter files, numbered sequentially.
- **Back-Matter:** Appendices, endnotes, bibliography, index notes, about the author.
- **Style-Guide:** Writing style, formatting standards, and voice/tone guidelines.
- **Editorial-Guidelines:** Standards and processes for the editorial team.

### 50-Sources-and-Contacts: Your Rolodex

Nonfiction writing involves real people. This directory manages your relationships with the experts, subjects, and sources who contribute to your book.

- **Experts:** Profiles of subject-matter experts you've consulted.
- **Interviewees:** Profiles of people you've interviewed, including attribution preferences.
- **Correspondence:** A log of outreach emails, letters, and follow-ups.
- **Permissions:** Tracking for quote permissions, image rights, and data usage clearances.

### 60-Team: Your Collaboration Hub

For projects involving editors, reviewers, agents, and publishers, the Team directory keeps everyone coordinated.

- **Editor:** Editor notes, feedback, and the editorial calendar.
- **Reviewers:** Reviewer assignments, feedback summaries, and review status.
- **Agent:** Agent notes and submission tracking.
- **Publisher:** Publisher communications and contract notes.
- **Fact-Checking:** Fact-check logs, verification checklists, and correction records.

### 70-Maps: Your Whiteboard

The Maps directory is for visual thinking and structural planning. It helps you see the big picture — how arguments connect to evidence, how concepts relate to each other, and how chapters flow together.

- **Argument-Maps:** How your main argument breaks down into supporting claims.
- **Concept-Maps:** Relationships between key ideas, themes, and topics.
- **Structure-Maps:** The architecture of the book and how chapters connect.
- **Evidence-Maps:** Which sources support which arguments, and where coverage gaps exist.

### 99-Templates: Your Foundation for Consistency

The Templates directory contains starting-point templates for every common document type in Spelunk Vault, from chapter drafts to interview notes to permission requests.

- **Chapter-Templates:** For chapter drafts and chapter plans.
- **Research-Templates:** For source notes, interview notes, field notes, and annotated sources.
- **Contact-Templates:** For expert profiles, interviewee profiles, and permission requests.
- **Meeting-Templates:** For editorial and research meetings.

## How to Begin Your Journey

1. **Set up your vault:** Clone or download Spelunk Vault and open it in Obsidian.
2. **Explore the templates:** Familiarize yourself with `99-Templates/` to understand the expected structure of your documents.
3. **Define your book:** Populate `10-Book-Bible/` with your thesis, key arguments, target audience, and scope.
4. **Organize your research:** Begin filing sources in `20-Research/` and adding them to the master bibliography.
5. **Build your outline:** Create your book outline in `30-Development/Outlines/` and draft chapter plans.
6. **Log your progress:** Use the Writing Log template in `00-Project-Management/Writing-Logs/` from day one.
7. **Start writing:** When you're ready, use the chapter template to begin drafting in `30-Development/Drafts/`, then move finalized chapters to `40-Manuscript/Chapters/`.

Spelunk Vault is designed to grow with your book. By following its structure, you can keep even the most research-heavy project organized, traceable, and under control. Happy spelunking!
